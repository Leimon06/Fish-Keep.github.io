Molly

Mollies, belonging to the Poecilia genus, are diverse live-bearers with striking varieties like the sailfin molly.
They exhibit vibrant colors and unique features.
Although generally hardy, they prefer warmer water.
Mollies can develop "shimmies" under environmental stress, not an infectious disease but a stress-related condition.













Species Overview

COMMON NAMES: Short-finned molly, sailfin molly, black molly, white molly, golden molly, lyretail molly, dalmatian molly
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Poecilia sp.
ADULT SIZE: Up to 5.5 inches
LIFE EXPECTANCY: 5 years

Origin and Distribution

Mollies, members of the Poecilia genus, are primarily found from Southern United States to Central America and northern South America.
Thriving in freshwater, they can adapt temporarily to brackish estuaries.
Mollies, closely related to guppies, share genetic ties and can interbreed under certain circumstances.

Colors and Markings

Captive mollie species, interbred for over a century, display diverse color varieties and fin shapes.
Common types include solid black mollies, sailfin mollies with orange-bordered dorsal fins, albino forms, lyre tails, and unique varieties like chocolate, gold dust, and orange mollies.
The sailfin mollies, such as Poecilia latipinna and P. velifera, have large dorsal fins.
Balloon mollies, a result of genetic defects, are selectively bred and prone to health issues but can still reproduce.

Tankmates

Mollies thrive with compatible community fish of similar size and environmental preferences,
such as cory catfish, danios, tetras, loaches, and barbs.
However, caution is advised with larger mollies around smaller creatures like cherry shrimp, as predation may occur.

Molly Goldfish Habitat and Care

Mollies are adaptable to various water conditions, thriving in freshwater with temperatures around 23-24°C (73-75°F),
but can also tolerate brackish and short periods in salty seawater.

Diet and Feeding

In nature, mollies are nearly exclusively plant and algae eaters,
They enjoy eating algae growth in the aquarium, and will graze endlessly on it, looking for choice patches to nibble.
If your aquarium lacks algae, you must provide “spirulina” flakes, special commercial Molly food, or small amounts of chopped boiled spinach.

Gender Differences

Mollies exhibit clear gender distinctions, with males possessing a gonopodium and large dorsal fins,
while females have a smaller dorsal fin and a fan-shaped anal fin.
Pregnant females showcase a noticeable swelling and a dark "gravid spot" near the anal fin.
Mollies are live-bearers, and the females release free-swimming fry,
but there's no parental care, and adults may eat the offspring if not hidden in aquatic plants.
Females give birth to a dozen or more fry monthly.

Breeding the Mollies

Livebearers, such as mollies, are prolific breeders, and with one male and one female,
expect fry in 30 to 60 days. Veteran moms can yield over 50 offspring.
To increase survival, provide dense plants for hiding. Fry are larger than those from eggs, consuming crushed flakes, gel food, and baby brine shrimp.
It takes four to nine months for them to reach juvenile size.
