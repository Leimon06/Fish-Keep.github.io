Barb

The very popular tiger barb is an easy fish to care for and
can be fun to watch as it swims at high speed in schools of six or more.
It is not, however, an ideal fish for a community tank as it can be mildly aggressive and nip any fish with flowing fins.
When well cared for, tiger barbs have a lifespan of five to seven years.












Species Overview

COMMON NAMES: Tiger barb, sumatra barb, and partbelt barb
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Barbus tetrazona
ADULT SIZE:3 inches
LIFE EXPECTANCY: 6 years

Characteristics

Family Cyprinidae
Origin Borneo, Indonesia, Sumatra
Social Active schooling fish, nips fins
Tank Level mid-dweller
Minimum Tank Size 20 gallon
Diet Omnivore, eats most foods
Breeding Egglayer
care Easy to Intermediate
pH 6.5
Hardness up to 10 dGH
Temperature 68 to 79 F (20 to 26 C)

Origin and Distribution

Tiger barbs originate from Borneo, Malaysia, Sumatra, Thailand, and Cambodia,
residing in tree-lined rivers with sand, rocks, and dense vegetation.
They prefer clear, oxygen-rich water and have been introduced in various countries outside their native range.

Colors and Markings

The tiger barb is known for its distinctive appearance, featuring four black vertical bands on an orange-yellow body,
complemented by red-edged fins and a red face.
Selective breeding has produced various color variations, including green, black, red, albino, and genetically modified glow versions in different colors.

Tankmates

Tiger barbs, reaching 2.5 to 3 inches in size, are suitable for a school in a moderate-sized aquarium.
A species-specific tank with various color variations can create a visually appealing display.
Compatible tankmates include clown loaches or other fast-moving species like danios, platys, loaches, or catfish.
It's essential to keep tiger barbs in groups of at least six to mitigate aggressive behavior.
Avoid pairing them with docile or slow-moving fish, as tiger barbs may become territorial and aggressive.

Barb Habitat and Care

Tiger barbs are adaptable to various water conditions but thrive in soft, slightly acidic water.
Create an ideal tank with ample open swimming space, surrounded by live or artificial plants.
Temperature is flexible, and they can be kept in unheated tanks.
Good lighting and a fine substrate complete the setup.

Diet and Feeding

Tiger barbs are versatile eaters, accepting various foods.
Provide a mix of quality pellets, flakes, live or frozen options like brine shrimp, bloodworms, and beef heart.
They also enjoy small aquatic invertebrates and cooked vegetables.

Gender Differences

Females have a broader, more rounded belly than males and are larger and heavier.
Males are identified by their bright coloring and the red nose they develop during the spawning process.

Breeding the Barb

To breed tiger barbs, set up a separate tank with soft, acidic water, fine-leaved plants, and a bare bottom or spawning grid.
Condition the pair with live foods, then move them to the breeding tank.
After spawning, remove the parents to prevent egg consumption.
The eggs hatch in about 36 hours, and fry become free-swimming in five days.
Feed them newly hatched brine shrimp until they can take finely crushed flakes or powdered fry food.
