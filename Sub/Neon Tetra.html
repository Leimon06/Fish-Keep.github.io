Neon Tetra

The dazzling crown jewel of aquariums everywhere,
the hardy little neon tetra fish was originally imported from South America.
Neon tetras must be kept in groups of at least a half-dozen, as they are a shoaling species.
With peaceful dispositions, they are also able to be kept with other species of non-aggressive fish.
They have a decently long life expectancy of 5 or more years.












Species Overview

COMMON NAMES: Neon tetra, neon fish
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Paracheirodon innesi
ADULT SIZE: 1.5 inches (4 centimeters)
LIFE EXPECTANCY: 5 to 10 years

Characteristics

Family Characidae
Origin Southeastern Colombia, eastern Peru, western Brazil
Social Peaceful
Tank Level Mid-dweller
Minimum Tank Size 10 gallon
Diet Omnivore
Breeding Egg scatterer
care Intermediate
pH 7
Hardness Up to 10 dGH
Temperature 68 to 79 F (20 to 26 C)

Origin and Distribution

Neon tetras originate from the Orinoco and Amazon river basins in Brazil, Columbia, and Peru, dwelling in dark, clear streams beneath forest canopies.
They live in shoals, mainly in middle water layers, feeding on insects, worms, and small crustaceans.
While generally captive-bred, originating from fish farms in the Far East and Eastern Europe,
various varieties are available, including long-finned, golden, diamond (with metallic scales), and a blue-striped variety lacking red coloration.

Colors and Markings

The neon tetra, with a slender body reaching up to 1.5 inches, boasts vibrant colors.
A neon blue stripe runs from nose to adipose fin, aiding visibility in blackwater conditions.
Below, a white-silver belly contrasts with a bright red stripe extending to the tail. This red, white, and blue combination makes them a popular aquarium fish,
often confused with the cardinal tetra, distinguished by a red stripe running the entire length.
Neon tetras' colors may fade at rest or when alarmed.
Choose specimens with robust coloring for optimal health.

Tankmates

Neon tetras are peaceful and thrive in groups of half a dozen or more.
They are ideal for community tanks with small, peaceful companions like rasboras, dwarf gouramis, and corys.
Avoid larger or aggressive species, as neon tetras can be vulnerable to predation.
It's crucial to keep them with fish whose mouths cannot fit a neon tetra to ensure their safety.

Neon Tetra Habitat and Care

Neon tetras thrive in fully matured aquariums with stable water chemistry.
Optimal conditions include soft, acidic water (pH below 7.0, hardness no more than 10 dGH).
Darkening the water with blackwater extracts or driftwood mimics their natural habitat.
Provide plenty of low-light hiding places with plants, including floating ones.
Driftwood and a dark substrate contribute to a comfortable environment,
their natural habitat with dark water, dense vegetation, and roots.
A dark background on three sides enhances the desired low-light setting.

Diet and Feeding

Neon tetras, natural omnivores, thrive on fine flakes, small granules, and live or frozen brine shrimp, daphnia, and bloodworms.
Feed them 2-4 times daily, providing an amount they can consume in two minutes to avoid overfeeding.
Variety in their diet, including live foods, ensures good health.
Remove any leftover food promptly to prevent high ammonia levels,
which can be harmful to neon tetras.

Gender Differences

Gender differences are not overtly apparent in neon tetras.
Generally, the female will have a larger more rounded belly than the male.
This rounded belly can even make the blue stripe appear curved on the female,
in contrast to the very straight blue stripe on the male.

Breeding the Neon Tetra

To breed neon tetras, set up a separate tank with soft water (1-2 dGH) and acidic pH (5.0-6.0).
Use a sponge filter, live plants, and cover the tank with dark paper.
Keep the temperature between 72-75°F (22-24°C).
Condition the pair with live foods, introduce them gradually to lighting, and spawn usually occurs in the morning.
The male embraces the female, laying over 100 eggs on plants.
Remove the parents immediately to prevent egg consumption.
Maintain low lighting for sensitive eggs and fry.
Hatch rates are modest, with one-third developing into viable fry.
Feed the fry small foods like infusoria and gradually progress to freshly hatched brine shrimp.
Adult coloration appears after a month.
