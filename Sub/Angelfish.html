Angel Fish

Angelfish belong to the cichlid family and are available in two types: freshwater and saltwater.
This guide focuses on freshwater angelfish,
known for their distinctive arrowhead bodies and flowing fins. While generally peaceful,
they may prey on small fish and exhibit aggression,
particularly during the breeding season.












Species Overview

COMMON NAMES: Angelfish
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Pterophyllum
ADULT SIZE: 3-4 inches
LIFE EXPECTANCY: 10-12 years

Characteristics

Breeding Egg layer

Origin and Distribution

Angelfish are native to tropical South America, inhabiting slow-moving streams,
floodplains, and swamps within the Amazon water system.
They thrive in warm, acidic water with no salinity,
preferring a habitat with sandy riverbeds, vegetation, and clear water.

Colors and Markings

Angelfish are characterized by wide bodies, triangular snouts, and long, flowing fins.
Commonly silver with four vertical black stripes, they may also come in gold, black, white, orange, yellow, marbled, albino, platinum, gold, and panda varieties.
The number and brightness of stripes and colors can vary based on the fish's maturity, mood, and stress levels.

Tankmates

In the wild, angelfish coexist with thousands of other fish in diverse habitats.
In a tank, their territorial nature can lead to aggression,
so it's best to avoid housing them with shy species.
Good tank mates include other angelfish (up to six in a spacious tank),
compatible cichlids, dwarf gouramis, mollies, plecos, and pictus catfish.
Avoid keeping them with non-fish tank mates, as angelfish may harass or attack invertebrates like crabs and shrimp.

Angel fish Habitat and Care

Create a tank environment for angelfish that mimics their natural habitat in the slow-moving waters of the Amazon basin.
Use a tank of at least 20 gallons, with an additional 10 gallons per extra fish, soft substrate like sand or mud, and low-flow aeration to simulate their preferred current.
Mimic natural sunlight with an aquarium LED light for 8 to 12 hours daily.
Include native Amazonian plants such as Amazon sword and anacharis, along with sturdy options like Java fern and Java moss.
Provide caves, rocks, and hollow decorations for hiding and territorial spaces, avoiding floating plants.

Diet and Feeding

Angelfish thrive on a diet of small live prey like insects, larvae, crustaceans, and small fish in the wild.
In captivity, replicate this by offering a high-protein, high-fiber diet with limited plant matter.
Feed them live foods like tubifex worms, brine shrimp, and water fleas.
Additionally, provide high-protein fish flakes or pellets and a small amount of high-fiber plant food like blanched spinach or zucchini.
Feed at least twice a day, offering a pinch of food each time for a duration of two minutes, and promptly remove any uneaten food to maintain tank cleanliness.

Gender Differences

Male angelfish are sleeker with pointed fins and develop a small papilla.
Females have a rounder body, shorter fins, and lack the papilla.
Males can be more territorial, especially during breeding.
Watch for these traits as they mature.

Breeding the Angel Fish

Breeding angel fish is relatively easy.
Once a pair naturally forms in your tank, set up a separate 20-gallon breeding tank with slow-flowing water, a temperature of 82°F, and a suitable spawning surface.
Condition the pair with a high-protein diet, move them to the breeding tank, and observe their behavior.
When the female lingers near the spawning surface, she's ready to lay eggs.
The male fertilizes the eggs, which hatch in two to three days.
The parents care for the fry for about a month; then, transfer them to a community tank after six weeks.
Feed the fry brine shrimp larvae initially, then transition to flakes and dried food.
