Fantail Goldfish

The fantail goldfish is a common variety of fancy goldfish, known for its double tail.
Rather than having one standard tail with a top and bottom caudal fin,
these goldfish have been bred to have two top and bottom caudal fins,
with the top fin being fused along the top.
Take special consideration when keeping fantail goldfish in order to accommodate their impaired swimming ability.












Species Overview

COMMON NAMES: Fantail goldfish, double tail goldfish
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Carassius auratus
ADULT SIZE: 7 inches
LIFE EXPECTANCY: 10 to 15 years

Characteristics

Origin and Distribution

While the original comet goldfish has its roots in the Crucian Carp of Asia,
the fantail goldfish is a product of human-driven selective breeding. As a type of "fancy" goldfish,
the fantail variety was meticulously developed over numerous generations by selectively pairing individuals with desired traits.

Colors and Markings

Fantail goldfish are known for their double tails,
but also tend to have short bodies and may have elongated pectoral and anal fins.
Depending on if your fantail has been bred with other varieties of fancy goldfish,
such as orandas, ryukins, or pearlscales, your fantail may take on some characteristics of those other goldfish,
such as a small wen (head swelling), tall body, or enlarged coelom.
Fantail goldfish are typically orange to white in color but can be red or multicolored if they have been crossed with a shubunkin or ryukin goldfish variety.

Tankmates

For fantail goldfish, the best tankmates are other fancy goldfish,
as long-bodied varieties may out-swim them. It's advisable to avoid adding any tankmates,
and if keeping a single fantail, provide a spacious,
filtered aquarium for optimal health and well-being.

Fantail Goldfish Habitat and Care

Fantail goldfish, known for their double tails, have short bodies and may exhibit elongated pectoral and anal fins.
They are generally easy-going and low-maintenance, like other goldfish varieties.
However, due to their less efficient swimming abilities, it's advisable not to overcrowd their environment with decorations.
Regular maintenance is crucial due to their messy nature. Fantails may rest more frequently,
so providing an open area on the substrate is beneficial for their comfort.
Depending on breeding with other fancy goldfish varieties,
they may show characteristics like a small wen, tall body, or enlarged coelom. While typically orange to white,
they can display red or multicolored patterns if crossed with shubunkin or ryukin goldfish.

Diet and Feeding

Choose the correct pellet size for your fantail goldfish, ensuring easy swallowing.
Opt for a pellet with around 32-35% protein, slightly higher for fancy goldfish due to their swimming difficulties.
Aim for a 4-5% fat content, and replace the food container every 6 months for sufficient vitamin C levels.
Sinking diets are recommended to prevent buoyancy issues.
Feed once a day below 70°F (21°C) and twice a day at 70°F and above.
Sprinkle enough food for a leisurely 5-minute meal,
removing any uneaten food to prevent waste accumulation.

Gender Differences

Like comet goldfish, fantail goldfish have distinguishable features for males.
Breeding tubercles, raised white bumps, will appear on the operculum
(gill cover) and leading edge of the pectoral fin in males during spawning season. emales tend to be more round,
but with the enlarged belly on Fantails, this is not reliable.

Breeding the Goldfish

To encourage goldfish breeding, boost protein and fat in their diet for full gonad development.
Place one male and one female fantail goldfish in a breeding tank, raise the temperature,
add live plants, and manipulate daylight length to simulate spring.
After spawning, remove the parents to prevent egg consumption.
Feed powdered fry food to the free-swimming babies.
