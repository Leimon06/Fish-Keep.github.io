Danios (Zebrafish)

Zebra danios are a favorite of freshwater fish hobbyists because of their ease of care.
They are also prolific breeders and the easiest type of egglayers to breed. With their attractively striped,
black and white zebra-patterned bodies, these fish are easy to recognize.
Zebrafish are very durable and can withstand an impressive range of water temperatures and conditions.
They will generally do just fine without a water heater as they are comfortable at temperatures down to the low 60s F.












Species Overview

COMMON NAMES: Striped danio, zebra danio, zebrafish
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Danio rerio
ADULT SIZE: 2 inches
LIFE EXPECTANCY: 5 years

Characteristics

Diet Omnivore

Origin and Distribution

Zebra danios were initially believed to have a broad range from Myanmar to Pakistan,
but current consensus places their origin in a narrower region in parts of India and Bangladesh.
This shift is due to the identification of similar species previously misidentified as Danio rerio.
Environmental damage has further restricted their natural habitat, though wild zebra danios occupy various environments.
Most zebra danios in the aquarium industry are now captive-bred, providing a large and affordable supply.
However, commercial breeding has led to a less robust version compared to the original wild species.
Numerous zebra danio varieties are available in aquarium stores.

Colors and Markings

The zebra danio is recognized by its distinct blue-purple horizontal stripes along its slim, silver-gold body.
Varieties include albino, golden, veil-tailed, long-finned, and the spotted leopard strain, once considered a different species.
Genetic studies confirmed it as a variation of Danio rerio. The GloFish, a fluorescent variety with introduced DNA pigment, displays bright colors like orange, green, blue, purple, or red while retaining the characteristic stripes.

Tankmates

Zebra danios, with a small size of no more than two inches, are ideal for community aquariums.
Peaceful but active, they may nip fins of fish with long-flowing fins.
Suitable tankmates include similar-sized danios, barbs, corydoras catfish, tetras, loaches, mollies, platys, and swordtails.
Thriving in schools of five or more, they establish a pecking order without fighting.
Choose tankmates with similar temperaments and energy levels, avoiding mellow fish that may become stressed in their active presence.

Zebra Danio Habitat and Care

Zebras are primarily surface-dwelling, cold-water fish that prefer temperatures between 64 to 75°F but can adapt to various conditions.
They become more susceptible to disease in lower temperatures.
Highly active, they explore the entire tank, favoring the upper levels.
Provide diffuse lighting, an open swimming space, and vegetation around the tank's periphery.
A darker substrate enhances their colors and adds a natural feel to their habitat.

Diet and Feeding

Zebra danios are omnivorous, accepting almost any food. Although undemanding in diet,
they particularly enjoy small, live, or frozen invertebrates and fresh vegetable matter.
They can be fed small pelleted food or flake fish food for their main diet,
with supplements of freeze dried bring shrimp or tubifex worms, or frozen foods.

Gender Differences

Both sexes have two pairs of barbels and the same stripes, but females are usually larger.
Males are a bit smaller and more slender than females.
Male zebras appear more torpedo-shaped, while females tend to have a larger belly.
More full-bodied than the males, a female's belly balloons when it fills with eggs.

Breeding the Zebra Danio


Zebra danios are ideal for beginners due to their prolific breeding.
Females can spawn every two to three days after reaching maturity at around four months.
To breed them, start with a school of young zebras, watch for females with rounded bellies, and separate them into a breeding tank with fine-leafed plants.
Use a coarse substrate to protect the eggs from adult fish.
Spawning is induced by a water change and requires temperatures of 78 to 80°F.
After laying 300 to 500 eggs, remove the breeders.
Fry hatch in about two days, needing care with water changes and a sponge filter to prevent suction.
Feed them commercially prepared fry food or finely crushed flakes, avoiding overfeeding to prevent water fouling.
